# Maternal Health Analytics Dashboard

## Project Overview

This project analyzes maternal healthcare indicators across Indian states using the Rural Health Statistics (RHS) 2021–22 dataset. The dashboard provides insights into institutional delivery, maternal mortality ratio (MMR), antenatal care (ANC-4 coverage), and healthcare workforce availability, helping identify states that require policy attention and resource allocation.

## Business Problem

Maternal healthcare outcomes vary significantly across Indian states due to differences in healthcare infrastructure, workforce availability, and access to maternal health services.

Healthcare administrators and policymakers require an interactive dashboard to identify high-performing and low-performing states, monitor workforce shortages, and support data-driven decisions for improving maternal healthcare.

## Objectives

- Analyze institutional delivery rates across Indian states.
- Compare maternal mortality ratio (MMR) between states.
- Evaluate ANC-4 coverage.
- Identify doctor, specialist, and nursing workforce shortages.
- Compare required vs available healthcare professionals.
- Provide insights for improving maternal healthcare planning.

## Dataset

Source:
Rural Health Statistics (RHS) 2021–22

Dataset includes:

- State
- Institutional Delivery (%)
- Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR)
- ANC-4 Coverage (%)
- Doctors Required
- Doctors In Position
- Specialist Required
- Specialist In Position
- Nursing Required
- Nursing In Position
- Workforce Shortfalls

## Business Insights

### 1. High-Performing States in Institutional Delivery

Kerala, Goa, Lakshadweep, Puducherry, and Tamil Nadu recorded the highest institutional delivery rates. These states demonstrate strong maternal healthcare performance and can serve as benchmarks for improving maternal health services in other regions.

### 2. States Requiring Attention for Institutional Delivery

Nagaland, Meghalaya, Jharkhand, Bihar, and Arunachal Pradesh reported the lowest institutional delivery rates. Lower institutional delivery may indicate limited access to healthcare facilities, lower awareness, or infrastructure challenges that require targeted policy interventions.

### 3. Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR)

Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, and Uttarakhand recorded the highest Maternal Mortality Ratios. These states require focused efforts to improve maternal healthcare services, emergency obstetric care, and early pregnancy monitoring.

### 4. States with Better Maternal Health Outcomes

Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, and Gujarat recorded the lowest Maternal Mortality Ratios. Their healthcare systems may provide useful practices that can be studied and adapted in other states.

### 5. ANC-4 Coverage

Nagaland, Bihar, Arunachal Pradesh, Jharkhand, Uttar Pradesh, and Assam reported the lowest ANC-4 coverage. Lower antenatal care coverage increases the risk of undetected pregnancy complications and highlights the need for stronger maternal healthcare outreach programs.

### 6. Healthcare Workforce Shortages

The dashboard highlights significant shortages of doctors, specialists, and nursing staff across several states. Workforce shortages remain one of the major challenges affecting maternal healthcare delivery.

### 7. Gap Between Required and Available Doctors

The comparison between doctors required and doctors currently in position shows that many states have not yet met the recommended healthcare workforce requirements. Reducing this gap is essential for improving healthcare accessibility and maternal outcomes.

### 8. State-wise Performance Variation

The analysis shows considerable variation across states in institutional delivery, maternal mortality, ANC coverage, and healthcare workforce availability. This indicates that maternal healthcare challenges differ by region and require state-specific planning rather than a uniform national approach.

## Business Recommendations

### 1. Improve Institutional Delivery in Low-Performing States

States such as Nagaland, Meghalaya, Jharkhand, Bihar, and Arunachal Pradesh should strengthen awareness campaigns, improve access to healthcare facilities, and encourage institutional deliveries through community outreach programs.

### 2. Strengthen Antenatal Care (ANC) Services

States with low ANC-4 coverage should prioritize regular antenatal check-ups, increase awareness among pregnant women, and improve healthcare accessibility to ensure early detection of pregnancy-related complications.

### 3. Address Healthcare Workforce Shortages

Recruitment and retention of doctors, specialists, and nursing staff should be prioritized in states experiencing significant workforce shortages. Adequate staffing is essential for improving maternal healthcare outcomes.

### 4. Learn from High-Performing States

Healthcare practices followed by Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Goa, and other high-performing states should be studied and adapted where feasible to improve maternal healthcare delivery in lower-performing regions.

### 5. Focus on High MMR States

States with higher Maternal Mortality Ratios such as Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, and Uttarakhand should receive additional investments in maternal healthcare infrastructure, emergency obstetric services, and skilled birth attendance.

### 6. Use Data-Driven Monitoring

Regular monitoring of institutional delivery, ANC coverage, maternal mortality, and workforce availability through interactive dashboards can support evidence-based decision-making and improve healthcare planning.

## Project Limitations

- The analysis is based on state-level data and does not capture district-level or local variations in maternal healthcare performance.
- The dataset represents a single reporting period and does not allow trend or time-series analysis.
- The analysis focuses on available healthcare indicators and does not include socio-economic factors such as education, income, transportation, or rural–urban differences, which may also influence maternal health outcomes.
- The dashboard identifies patterns and relationships but does not establish cause-and-effect relationships between variables.
- The quality of insights depends on the accuracy and completeness of the source dataset.

## Future Scope

- Extend the analysis using district-level healthcare data for more detailed regional insights.
- Incorporate multiple years of data to perform trend and time-series analysis.
- Combine maternal healthcare data with socio-economic indicators such as literacy, income, and population density for deeper analysis.
- Develop predictive models using Machine Learning to estimate Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR) based on healthcare indicators.
- Publish the dashboard through Power BI Service for real-time monitoring and wider accessibility.

## Conclusion

This project demonstrates how Power BI can be used to analyze maternal healthcare indicators and transform raw healthcare data into meaningful insights. By examining institutional delivery rates, Maternal Mortality Ratio (MMR), ANC-4 coverage, and healthcare workforce availability, the dashboard highlights both high-performing states and areas requiring policy attention.

The analysis emphasizes the importance of strengthening maternal healthcare services, improving workforce availability, and promoting data-driven decision-making. The interactive dashboard enables healthcare administrators and policymakers to monitor key performance indicators efficiently and identify opportunities for improving maternal healthcare outcomes across Indian states.
